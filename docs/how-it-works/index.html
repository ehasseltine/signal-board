Everything here is open. Here's exactly how it works.

1

Sources

Every day, Signal Board reads RSS feeds from 112 news sources. These sources were selected to span the full American spectrum: national outlets across the political range including the New York Times, Fox News, NPR, The Federalist, and Mother Jones; international press such as BBC, Al Jazeera, Deutsche Welle, and The Guardian; community outlets serving specific populations like The Advocate, Indian Country Today, Capital B News, AsAmNews, Jewish Telegraphic Agency, and Arab American News; specialist publications including Inside Climate News, War on the Rocks, and Just Security; explainer outlets such as Vox, The Conversation, and 404 Media; and research organizations from multiple ideological perspectives.

Every source is listed on the Sources page with a description of what it is and why it's included.

2

Classification

Each article is tagged into one or more of 10 domains: AI, Labor, Governance, Information, Economics, Climate, Security, Geopolitics, Politics, and Legal. Classification uses AI (Claude by Anthropic) with keyword verification as a fallback.

Articles that touch multiple domains are tagged as cross-domain. Those intersections are where the most important patterns tend to emerge. When you see labor and AI tagged together, or governance and information overlapping, you're looking at stories that reveal structural shifts across domains.

3

Analysis

Every day, the system runs a narrative analysis that looks for where sources from different tiers and perspectives converge on the same story. It tracks which domain intersections are gaining or losing momentum over time. It identifies which stories are only being covered by community or specialist sources, revealing what mainstream outlets are missing. And it shows how coverage emphasis shifts depending on whether a source is national, international, community-based, or domain-specific.

This reveals the shape of the information landscape in ways that reading individual outlets never could.

4

What You See

The Today page shows the daily analysis in plain language: the stories that matter, why they're connected, what the source variety reveals, and which threads are building over time.

The Stories page has every article, filterable by domain. The Sources page holds every outlet accountable by showing what it is, what tier it belongs to, and why it's included.

What This Doesn't Do

Signal Board does not tell readers what to think, assign ideological scores to sources, or claim to be neutral. Neutrality is a pretense. What it does instead is show the broadest possible range of reporting on the same set of events and let the data reveal what the coverage patterns look like.

The goal is to help people see connections that fragmented media makes invisible. Signal Board surfaces structural patterns in coverage across ideological and geographic divides. It shows you which outlets are talking about the same events and which are talking past each other. It reveals which stories are being amplified across the spectrum and which are only reaching specific communities. And it does this without claiming that any single interpretation is correct.

You're the one who makes sense of the patterns. That's the whole point.
