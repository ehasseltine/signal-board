The Sources Behind Signal Board

People have good reason to be skeptical of news curation. In a media landscape saturated with bias and financial incentives, asking someone to trust your source list requires transparency about what you're actually doing. Signal Board doesn't claim to show you the "truth" or pick the outlets we think are right. Instead, we pull from 239 sources organized into eight functional categories, each chosen because it brings a distinct perspective to how stories develop and spread. You can see exactly which outlets we read, why they matter, and what different vantage points they represent. The goal is to help you understand not just what happened, but how the conversation around it is shaped.

How we categorize sources

Signal Board's eight categories organize sources by function, not by quality or reliability. Each category captures a distinct role that sources play in the news ecosystem, from setting the national agenda to reporting stories that never reach major outlets.

Our taxonomy operates along three primary dimensions. Geographic position describes where sources report from: some focus exclusively on the United States, while others cover the world from outside American perspectives. Topic focus describes what they cover: some outlets cover everything, while others concentrate deeply on specific domains like labor or climate. Format describes how they deliver: we include daily wire services, weekly newsletters, daily podcasts, and real-time government records all in one system.

This taxonomy is Signal Board's own framework, not an industry standard. We built it this way because existing journalism hierarchies often imply that some sources are inherently more credible than others, which obscures the real question: what perspective does this source bring, and is that perspective useful right now? A government primary document might be more authoritative on what Congress passed, while a community newsroom might be more authoritative on how that law affects a specific neighborhood. Both matter. Both belong in the same conversation.

U.S. Newsrooms

28 sources

Major American news organizations that shape the mainstream narrative and reach the largest audiences. These outlets set the agenda that most Americans encounter first, which makes understanding their framing essential to understanding how stories travel.

Global Press

40 sources

International news organizations reporting from outside the United States. These outlets cover events that American media underreports and frame shared events through different cultural, political, and economic lenses.

Community Voices

79 sources

Local newsrooms, outlets serving specific communities, and regional accountability journalism. These sources report stories that never reach national headlines: school board decisions, infrastructure failures, immigration enforcement, and the lived experience of policy changes.

Deep Expertise

38 sources

Specialist outlets focused on single domains like technology, labor, climate, defense, or health. These sources provide the technical depth and historical context that generalist newsrooms often lack.

Explainers

9 sources

Outlets whose primary function is translating complex policy, science, and institutional processes into language anyone can follow without sacrificing accuracy.

Research and Analysis

25 sources

Think tanks, research institutes, and academic publications producing data-driven analysis of policy and global affairs. Signal Board treats these as context sources, not news sources.

Newsletters and Podcasts

19 sources

Independent writers and audio producers who go deeper on fewer stories, often bringing subject expertise or original reporting that institutional outlets don't prioritize.

Government Sources

5 sources

Primary documents from Congress, federal agencies, and the courts. These are the record of what government is actually doing, before any outlet interprets it.

How we selected these sources

Our selection process prioritizes functional diversity over perceived objectivity. We ask what role each source plays in the information ecosystem, not whether we personally agree with its editorial stance. This means Signal Board includes outlets with clear ideological positions: Fox News and Mother Jones both appear because they both shape how millions of Americans understand the world, and understanding their coverage is essential to understanding the broader news cycle.

A source makes it into Signal Board if it meets one of three criteria. First, it reaches a significant audience and shapes how stories are framed and distributed. Second, it reports on a geographic region or topic with expertise and depth that few other outlets match. Third, it covers communities or stories that national media systematically ignores. We don't include sources because they're objective, because no source is. We include them because they're functionally important.

This source list has real limitations worth understanding. Coverage is weighted toward English-language outlets and outlets with digital presence, which skews toward wealthy countries and away from rural areas. Within the United States, we have stronger coverage of certain cities and weaker coverage of others. Some categories like Explainers are small because few outlets make that their primary function. And some domains get covered intensively while others, despite their importance, have sparse specialist coverage. These gaps exist. They matter. We try to name them.

Including a source in Signal Board does not mean endorsing its coverage, defending its editorial choices, or claiming it's reliable on every topic. It means recognizing that this outlet's voice contributes something to the broader conversation. The New York Times breaks investigative stories others miss, and it also frames some stories in ways shaped by its institutional interests. ProPublica does deep accountability work and has its own editorial judgment about what stories matter. Fox News reaches more conservative audiences than any other outlet and its coverage reflects conservative editorial priorities. These facts are all true simultaneously. Signal Board's job is to show you multiple voices talking about the same events, not to declare some voices acceptable and others illegitimate.

All 239 sources are monitored for new content daily. Some publish continuously; others update once a week or publish only on major news events. Signal Board treats all sources as equal participants in the conversation about what's happening, regardless of how frequently they publish. The focus is not on which outlet published first, but on which outlets published at all and what different outlets chose to cover.
