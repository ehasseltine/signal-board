What is Signal Board?

Every single day, Signal Board reads 275 news sources and asks seven questions about what it finds. Then it publishes what it learned.

It's built to show you something nobody else shows you: how the same structural forces move across outlets that claim they're separate. One company owns a newspaper. Another owns a streaming service. A third funds independent outlets through a nonprofit. Tech oligarchs don't just own news — they own the pipes, the money, and the distribution. That's a system. Signal Board exists to show how it works.

How it reads the news

Those 275 sources aren't all the same. Signal Board reads across 10 tiers: national outlets, legacy media, digital-first journalism, nonprofit newsrooms, conservative media, liberal media, international outlets, local-regional papers (79 of them), and ethnic and state-focused publications (31 sources built by and for specific communities).

Every morning, Signal Board scans what all of them published overnight. It looks for patterns. It asks questions:

Who is thinking well together? Which outlets are covering the same story and actually saying something true about it?

What's the information architecture? Who gets to decide which stories matter today?

Where does this connect to another domain? Is a story about tech actually a story about labor, or power, or health?

What does it look like for someone inside it? What would you see if you lived in this story instead of reading about it?

What would the honest version sound like? What's true here that the outlets aren't saying directly?

If you could tell someone one thing? What's the core fact that changes everything?

Where are people being decent? Where's the cooperation? Where's the kindness? Where's the real work happening?

Why this exists

For decades, news was a standalone business. A newspaper owned by a family or a public company, making money from readers and advertisers. It wasn't perfect. But ownership and operations and distribution were separate things.

That's broken. Now the same company that owns a news outlet also owns the platform that decides whether anyone sees it. That company sells advertising to the same companies it covers. It funds journalists through nonprofits (which then have to stay friendly). It buys other outlets when they become inconvenient. Ownership, funding, and distribution are now one system.

Signal Board exists to show you how that system actually works. Not to tell you what to think. Not to have an opinion. But to connect the dots so you can see the whole picture — the forces, the patterns, the power.

That's what it means to counter media capture. It's not about left versus right. It's about whether people who live in a democracy can actually see what's happening to the information they depend on.
